African-American Literature

The history of African-American literature reflects the progression of black cultural,
intellectual, and artistic expression through vast contributions of writing throughout time.

Authors

Phillis Wheatley

A Pioneering Voice

Phillis Wheatley, the first published African American writer, stands as a powerful symbol of the early progression of Black expression.

Despite being enslaved and denied formal education, Wheatley's mastery of language and profound themes in her poetry shattered stereotypes about Black intellectual ability.

Her work paved the way for future generations of Black writers, demonstrating the vast potential for cultural and intellectual contributions within African-American literature.

Olaudah Equiano

Words From the Middle Passage

Olaudah Equiano, a formerly enslaved person turned author, embodies the power of Black expression in the face of unimaginable hardship.

His autobiography, "The Interesting Narrative of the Life of Olaudah Equiano, or Gustavus Vassa, the African," stands as a crucial first-hand account of the horrors of the slave trade.

Equiano's eloquent and detailed narrative not only documented the brutality of slavery but also showcased his intelligence, resilience, and humanity.

Through his writing, Equiano gave voice to the voiceless, contributing significantly to the progression of Black expression and the fight for abolition.
