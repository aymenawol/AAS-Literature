Olaudah Equiano

Equiano, originally from modern day Nigeria, was kidnapped into slavery at age 11, transported to Barbados, Virginia, then bought by a merchant and taken to England.

He was traded between masters, fought in wars, bought his freedom, settled in England, and joined the abolitionist movement.

His autobiography The Interesting Narrative of the Life of Olaudah Equiano was published at 44, and became highly influential in abolitionist rhetoric.

Equiano's Narrative

Olaudah Equiano's autobiography is a condemnation of the slave trade.

Penned by Equiano himself, a formerly enslaved individual, the narrative exposes the horrors of slavery and the longing for freedom. It also showcases Equiano's journey towards self-education despite the oppressive circumstances.

The book gained widespread acclaim, becoming a cornerstone of the abolitionist movement and challenging misconceptions about the intellect and humanity of enslaved Africans. It paved the way for future slave narratives and inspired marginalized communities to advocate for their rights and dignity.

Frederick Douglass

Frederick Douglass, born into slavery, endured harsh conditions and cruelty on Colonel Lloyd's plantation.

Despite obstacles, he taught himself to read, becoming aware of the abolitionist movement and resolving to escape to the North.

He escaped to New York, changed his name to Douglass, and became a prominent figure in the abolitionist movement, advocating for the end of slavery and the rights of all people.

He wrote his influential autobiography, "Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave," becoming a prominent figure in the abolitionist movement, advocating for the end of slavery and the rights of all people.

Douglass's Narrative

Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass is a memoir and discourse on slavery and abolition published in 1845.

Douglass recounts his brutal experiences on a Maryland plantation and his determination to educate himself despite slavery's challenges.

The narrative follows Douglass's escape from slavery and his advocacy for emancipation.

Through vivid prose, Douglass exposes slavery's dehumanizing effects and challenges racial inequalities.

Harriet Jacobs

Born into slavery in Edenton, North Carolina around 1813 and escaped to the North in 1842, living a fugitive life for years.

Actively involved in the abolitionist movement before and after the Civil War.

Published her autobiography, Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl, published at 44.

Jacob's Narrative

Harriet Jacobs, writing as Linda Brent, recounts her life as a slave, facing abuse from her master, Dr. Flint.

To protect herself, she enters into a relationship with another white man, Mr. Sands.

Becoming a mother to two children, Benny and Ellen, she hides them in an attic for seven years to evade slavery's horrors.

She demonstrates resilience and hope in her determination to secure her children's freedom

Booker T. Washington

Born into slavery in Franklin County, Virginia, in 1856.

Attends the Hampton Institute after emancipation, where he learns the values of hard work, selflessness, and vocational education under the mentorship of General Samuel C. Armstrong.

Establishes the Tuskegee Institute in Alabama, emphasizing practical skills alongside academics and gains fame for his "Atlanta compromise" speech advocating for economic advancement for African Americans.

Booker T. Washington wrote his autobiography, Up from Slavery, published in 1901.

Washington's Narrative

Booker T. Washington recounts his journey from a plantation in Virginia to becoming a prominent educator and leader.

The book details his journey from being born into slavery in Virginia around 1856 to becoming a prominent educator.

Washington's experiences at Hampton and later as a teacher shape his belief in the value of hard work, selflessness, and vocational education for racial uplift.

He emphasizes the importance of education and hard work for racial advancement.
